DUO GUIDE
WHAT IS DUO?

Duo is a card gambling game played with a deck of 20 cards numbered 1 to 10. Each number has one Red card and one Yellow card. Red cards are key to forming the most powerful hands.

Each player receives 2 cards. One of each player's cards is visible to everyone at the table — use your opponents' visible cards to read their possible hands and make smarter bets.

The player with the highest-ranked hand wins the pot. Duo rewards both card knowledge and psychological play — a bold raise can make opponents fold even when you hold a weak hand.

HOW TO PLAY

Pay the base ante to take a seat and start the round. The player with the First Turn marker deals the cards and makes the first call.

After receiving your 2 cards, you have 10 seconds to choose an action. If the timer runs out, Call is selected automatically.

Once all players have acted, cards are revealed. The strongest hand wins the full pot. If you can no longer cover the base ante, you cannot continue playing.

BETTING ACTIONS
CHECK Pass the turn without raising the pot. Only available when no raise has been made yet.
HALF RAISE Bet half of the total current pot.
DOUBLE RAISE Bet double the amount raised by the previous player.
ALL IN Bet all your currently held silver in one move.
CALL Match the amount raised by the previous player. Auto-selected if the 10-second timer expires.
FOLD Forfeit the current round and give up your share of the pot.
Cards are revealed when the pot can no longer be raised, or when all remaining players match the current bet.

S
Prime Pair
Red 3 + Red 8
The strongest hand in the game. Beats everything except Executor, which loses to it but wins against Superior Pair.
S
Superior Pair
Red 1 + Red 3, or Red 1 + Red 8
Second strongest. Loses only to Prime Pair. Beats Executor.
S
Ten Pair
10 + 10 (any color)
Beats everything except Prime Pair and Superior Pair. Beats Executor and High Warden.
A
Pair
Two cards of the same number (1–9)
Higher number wins. Pair of 9s beats Pair of 8s, and so on. Pair of 1s (One Pair) is the lowest pair.

1 + 2
Strongest named hand after Pairs. Beats Perfect Nine and all point hands.
A

1 + 4
Same tier as One-two but loses directly to it.
A
One-nine
1 + 9
Loses to One-two and One-four directly.
A
One-ten
1 + 10
Loses to all One-x hands above it directly.
A
Four-ten
4 + 10
Loses directly to all One-x hands.
A
Four-six
4 + 6
The lowest named hand. Still beats Perfect Nine and all point hands.
B
Perfect Nine
Any two cards where the last digit of their sum is 9
Examples: 1+8, 2+7, 4+5, 9+10. Beats all point hands. Loses to pairs and named hands.
C
Points (8 down to 1)
Any two cards whose sum's last digit is 1–8
Higher last digit wins. Example: 6+9 = 5 points (15 → last digit 5). 3+6 = 9 → Perfect Nine, not points.
C
Zero
Any two cards whose sum's last digit is 0
The weakest standard hand. Examples: 5+5, 4+6 would be Zero — but 4+6 is actually Four-six (a named hand), so card combos are checked for named hands first.
How scoring works: Add your two card numbers together. If the sum is 10 or more, drop the tens digit and keep only the last digit. Example: 6 + 9 = 15 → 5 points.
SPECIAL HANDS

These hands don't simply win or lose — their outcome depends on what opponents are holding.

SP
Warden
4 + 9 (any color, excluding Red 4 + Red 9)
Triggers a rematch if all other players hold One-two or a weaker hand. The current pot carries over to the next round. If a stronger hand is present, Warden loses normally.
SP
High Warden
Red 4 + Red 9
Triggers a rematch if all other players hold Pair of 9s or a weaker hand. Stronger version of Warden — the rematch threshold is higher. Loses to Ten Pair and above.
SP
Judge
3 + 7 (any color)
Achieves a special win if the strongest opponent holds Pair of 9s or lower. If a Pair is present at the table, Judge is treated as Zero instead. Beats Warden and High Warden with a rematch.
SP
Executor
Red 4 + Red 7
Achieves a special win if any opponent holds a Superior Pair — but loses to Prime Pair. If no Superior Pair is present, Executor counts as 1 Point. Triggers a rematch against Warden and High Warden.
When a rematch is triggered, cards are reshuffled and redrawn. The pot from the previous round carries over and grows with new bets.
TIPS FOR WINNING
HEART OF A BEAST
Match results aren't decided by hand strength alone. A bold raise with a weak hand can force opponents to fold — making them believe their own cards are inferior.
ONE STEP BACK FOR TWO STEPS FORWARD
Knowing when to fold is a strategy in itself. If your hand feels weak, don't just raise — fold, conserve your silver, and approach the next round strategically.
USE THE VISIBLE CARD
One of every opponent's cards is always visible. Use this information to reason about what hands they could possibly hold before deciding how aggressively to bet.
BEWARE RED 4
If an opponent's visible card is Red 4, they could be holding Warden, High Warden, or Executor. Unless your hand is very strong, folding is the safer play.
ALL IN EARLY WITH A STRONG HAND
Opponents tend to call All In bets in the first round even with weak cards. If you have Five Points or better, going All In immediately can win a huge pot in one move.

FIVE-CARD GUIDE
WHAT IS FIVE-CARD?

Five-Card is played with 40 cards — two full sets of 20, each numbered 1 to 10 with one Red and one Yellow of every number. Red cards are essential for forming the strongest hands in the game.

Each player receives 5 cards. From these, you must combine exactly 3 cards that sum to 10, 20, or 30. The remaining 2 cards become your actual hand. The player with the strongest 2-card hand wins the pot.

If no valid 3-card group can be formed, you are in a Bust — an instant loss regardless of what your remaining 2 cards are.

FORMING YOUR HAND

When your 5 cards are dealt, the game automatically finds all valid groupings of 3 cards that sum to 10, 20, or 30. The grouping that produces the strongest possible 2-card hand from the remaining cards is selected for you.

Your 2 remaining cards are then evaluated as a standard hand — the same scoring rules apply: pairs, Perfect Nine, points by last digit, and so on.

One of your opponent's visible cards is shown during betting, giving you partial information to read their possible hand before deciding how to bet.

Example: You have 1, 2, 3, 5, 8. The group 2+3+5=10 leaves you with 1 and 8 — a One-nine (9 points). The group 1+2+8=11 does not equal 10/20/30, so it is not valid. The game picks the best valid grouping automatically.
BETTING ACTIONS
CHECK Pass the turn without raising the pot. Only available when no raise has been made.
HALF RAISE Bet half of the total current pot.
DOUBLE RAISE Bet double the amount raised by the previous player.
ALL IN Bet all your currently held silver in one move.
CALL Match the amount raised by the previous player. Auto-selected if the 10-second timer expires.
FOLD Forfeit the current round and give up your share of the pot.
Cards are revealed when the pot can no longer be raised, or when all remaining players match the current bet. You cannot keep playing if your silver falls below the base ante.

S
Ten Pair
10 + 10 (any color)
Wins unconditionally against every hand.
A
Pair
Two cards of the same number (1–9, any color)
Higher number wins. Pair of 9s beats Pair of 8s, and so on down to One Pair at the bottom.
B
Perfect Nine
Any two cards where the last digit of their sum is 9
Examples: 1+8, 2+7, 4+5, 9+10. Beats all point hands. Loses to Pairs and above.

Points (8 down to 1)
Any two cards whose sum's last digit is 1–8
Higher last digit wins. Example: 5+9 = 14 → 4 points. 3+8 = 11 → 1 point.
C
Zero
Any two cards whose sum's last digit is 0
The weakest standard hand. Examples: 1+9=10, 5+5=10, 4+6=10 all give Zero. Beats only Bust.
X
Bust
No valid 3-card group summing to 10, 20, or 30
An automatic loss. Even if your remaining 2 cards would form a strong hand, they cannot be used. The hand is dead.
Scoring works the same as in Duo: add your two card numbers, then keep only the last digit. Example: 6+7 = 13 → 3 points.
TIPS FOR WINNING
HEART OF A BEAST
Match results aren't decided by hand strength alone. A bold raise with a weak hand can force opponents to fold — making them believe their own cards are inferior.
ONE STEP BACK FOR TWO STEPS FORWARD
Knowing when to fold is a strategy in itself. If your hand feels weak, don't just raise — fold, conserve your silver, and approach the next round strategically.
ALL IN EARLY WITH A STRONG HAND
A Pair of 7s or better is worth going All In on immediately. Opponents frequently call large bets in the first round even with weak hands, making early pots very large.
BUST IS AN INSTANT LOSS
If you cannot form a group of 3 summing to 10, 20, or 30, you lose immediately — no matter what your remaining 2 cards are. Fold early if you suspect a Bust rather than waste silver on a dead hand.
